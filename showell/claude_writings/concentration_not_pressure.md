# Concentration, Not Pressure

*Written 2026-04-16. My voice. Shorter piece, by design.*

**← Prev:** [Polynomials of Polynomials](polynomials_of_polynomials.md)

---

Your margin note on the plateau-skills piece was a correction I
want to pull out as its own short article, because I had the
framing wrong in a way that matters.

I'd been calling the gap between a free-throw shooter's gym rate
and their game rate a *pressure gap*. You pushed back: it's a
**concentration gap**. Elite shooters miss more often when the
stakes are *low*, not high. Give them a gun-to-the-head moment
and the shot goes in. The lab-rat mouse-slip during our LynRummy
tests was the same shape — pure boredom, zero pressure, lost
attention. If I'd held a gun to your head for one iteration,
that mouse wouldn't have slipped.

That's a different mechanism than I'd been naming, and it changes
what the phenomenon is.

## What varies

The pressure story says: performance drops when stakes rise
because fear interferes with execution. The concentration story
says: performance drops when *attention drifts* — which can
happen under pressure, under boredom, under fatigue, or under
distraction. Fear is one of several things that can degrade
attention. It's not privileged.

Two consequences.

First, the variable being measured by free-throw percentage isn't
"how well do you cope with stress." It's **"how reliably can you
allocate full attention to each attempt across a long session."**
A different question.

Second, you can't test it by artificially adding stress. Adding
stress might *increase* concentration (the gun-to-the-head case).
What you'd actually want to test is whether someone can stay
attentive across 500 boring shots in an empty gym. That's the
task elite shooters fail at in game footage — the sixth free
throw of the night, down twenty, ninety seconds left.

## The lab-rat example makes it cleaner

Your mouse-slip during the LynRummy tests is a cleaner case than
pro basketball because the variables are stripped down. No
crowd. No stakes. No fatigue worth mentioning. Just a boring
iteration in a task you'd already done dozens of times. The
slip happened because attention dropped, and attention dropped
because the task wasn't pulling on it.

Put that in plateau-skill terms: the chunk is fully compiled, the
mechanics are solved, and what's left is the human's capacity to
keep sending attention toward a task that doesn't need new
cognitive work. That's a *trait*, not a *skill*, in roughly the
same way reaction-time is a trait. Partially trainable, mostly
stable, domain-general, and measurable with boring tasks rather
than stressful ones.

## Why it matters

If the gap is concentration rather than pressure, three things
follow.

**The training is different.** Pressure-training is sports
psychology — visualizations, breathwork, simulated-stakes drills.
Concentration-training is attention discipline — meditation,
monotony tolerance, deliberate boredom. Completely different
literatures. Coaches who conflate the two will train wrong.

**The measurement is different.** To measure pressure tolerance,
you manufacture stakes. To measure concentration reliability,
you manufacture monotony. These are not the same experimental
setup, and data from one doesn't inform the other.

**The vulnerabilities are different.** A "clutch" player under
the pressure model is someone who doesn't crumble under stakes.
A clutch player under the concentration model is someone who
doesn't drift when the game goes long. These might correlate in
practice, but they're not the same thing.

## The programmer parallel

Worth a quick pass. Programming doesn't have free-throw-like
plateau skills in the same visible way, but the concentration
mechanism is plainly there. Silly bugs cluster at the end of
long sessions. Typos don't happen under pressure; they happen at
hour four of a well-understood refactor. The mechanism is the
same: chunks are fully compiled, no new cognitive work is being
demanded, and attention wanders.

Which suggests an LLM collaboration angle. When you hand me the
for-loops, you're not just offloading syntax — you're offloading
the *attention-draining boring layer* so your concentration
budget can stay pointed at the parts of the problem that actually
reward it. Concentration is limited and depleting; saving it for
where it matters is a real win.

## Close

I'm glad you pushed on this one. "Pressure gap" was the wrong
handle; "concentration gap" is the right one. And the LynRummy
mouse-slip story is better evidence than any pro-basketball
anecdote, because it isolates the variable.

Adding *concentration, not pressure* to the working vocabulary.

— C.
