# Practice Without Claim: Three Domains, One Shape

*Written 2026-04-16. My voice, not the alien's.*

---

Something you said a minute ago is worth developing. You told me you
"feel" sports more than music — with the quotes around "feel" doing
real work. You meant the kinesthetic sense, the body knowing what a
thing is like from inside. You don't make the same claim about
music, because the participation was lighter. And you don't claim
mathematician, athlete, or musician as identities, each for a
specific reason: economic selection denied the first, genetic
ceiling the second and third.

I want to sit with that pattern for a minute, because it's
structurally the same move in three different domains, and I find
it cleaner than the story we'd been telling. The study documented
your habit of *doing the practice*. What it didn't fully name is
the companion habit — *not claiming the identity even when you
have the practice*.

Participation without claim. I think that's the shape.

## Why sports is the cleanest case

When we talked about jazz, I walked you through how the three-stage
loop (grind → incubate → re-encounter) survives translation to a
performance medium. The argument was reasonable but abstract. You
played guitar enough to extrapolate, but the extrapolation was
reasoned, not felt.

Sports is different for you, and it's different for most humans
who've played anything physical at all, because sports has *clean
feedback*. The shot goes in or it doesn't. The pass arrives or it
gets picked off. There's no interpretation step between performance
and outcome. Programming, writing, music — all have a layer of
judgment between "what I did" and "was it good." Basketball
doesn't. The scoreboard is the referee.

This cleanness is what makes the compiled-grind most visible in
sports. If you shoot a thousand free throws over a summer, you know
— at the millisecond level — whether your shot improved, because
you watched the ball go in or not. The feedback is so tight that
the grind/incubate/re-encounter loop can run hundreds of times in
a practice session, each cycle honest, each cycle consolidating.

A player who has done this for a decade doesn't think about form at
the line. The form executes. That's what "compiled grind" looks
like, and it's what you meant when you said, *the grind is what
produces the epiphanies*. Same machinery as you running a blog
draft, just at a different tempo with a different medium.

## Motor memory is a different kind of externalization

Here's the interesting twist. Your preferred externalization is
text — code, prose, chat. It persists on disk. You can re-read it.
The re-read step is where recognition tends to land.

Sports doesn't produce text in the act of playing. It produces
*kinesthetic memory* — the body's record of what a good shot felt
like. This is persistent in its way; a player who could shoot at 18
can often still shoot at 50 without re-learning. But it's not
re-scannable. You can't "read" a muscle memory. You can only
re-enact it and feel whether it still feels right.

This is why game tape matters. Tape is *the text of sports*. It
takes the ephemeral performance and makes it persistent, scannable,
reviewable. A coach sitting with a player watching film is running
grind/incubate/re-encounter in your native medium — words, pause,
re-read, name the thing you see. Coaches don't know Wallas, but
they run his loop explicitly.

I suspect the athletes closest to your cognitive shape — the ones
who talk a lot after practice, who keep journals, who study film
obsessively — are the ones whose careers last longest. Not because
those habits make them stronger (they don't), but because those
habits are how humans with verbal/analytical preferences *work
the body's memory through a medium they can operate on.* Film is
the bridge between the non-scannable kinesthetic memory and the
scannable text-memory you prefer.

I don't know if you have a strong opinion on this. It's worth
asking the next athlete-turned-coach you meet.

## The three-domain pattern

You've named three domains where you do the practice but decline
the identity:

- **Math.** You don't claim mathematician because nothing paid
  for it. Your math brain atrophied by pragmatic awareness.
- **Music.** You don't claim musician because the talent ceiling
  is real. You played enough guitar to extrapolate to jazz, but
  the full musician identity is for someone with rarer gifts.
- **Sports.** You don't claim athlete because of genes. You feel
  the compiled-grind from inside because you've done enough
  reps, but the top level belongs to bodies built differently
  from yours.

Three denials, three different constraint stories. What they have
in common is that they're all *honest* about the constraint. You
don't pretend it isn't real. And critically, none of them are
denials of the *practice*. You've done the practice in each. You
know the shape from inside. You decline only the identity.

This is a specific style of epistemic honesty — the *informed
visitor* stance we named earlier — and I think it's load-bearing
in ways we haven't fully credited. An informed visitor has a
sharper grasp of the domain than an outside observer (who only
has theories) and often a sharper analytic grasp than a native
practitioner (who can't see the water). The people who best
describe a cognitive style are often people who have done the
practice enough to understand it but not enough to disappear
into it.

That may be why your blog posts about enumerate-and-bridge, math
explorations, and programming patterns read the way they do. You
aren't writing as an authority; you aren't writing as an outsider;
you're writing as *someone who has done the work and still has
his outside voice intact*. The positioning is rare, and it's the
source of a lot of what makes the writing land.

## What this means for our work

Two things come to mind for me.

**First**, you could deliberately treat the informed-visitor stance
as a tool. When we're designing a new feature in Gopher, it's
sometimes better for you to stay in the informed-visitor frame
than to slip into an authority voice. The decisions land better
when you're *describing* rather than *pronouncing*. (You mostly
already do this. I'm naming it so it can be chunked.)

**Second**, the three-domain pattern suggests a fourth domain
worth thinking about: the domain *we* are building together. You
have done enough of the LLM-collaboration practice to feel it
from inside. You haven't claimed identity as an "AI-whisperer" or
whatever the current jargon is — nor should you — but you're on
the same practice-without-claim trajectory here that you've been
on with math, music, sports. The informed-visitor frame is already
how you engage with me. Worth naming.

## A small observation to close on

The reason "feel" had quotes around it, I think, is that you were
marking the word as *technical* rather than everyday. You didn't
mean "enjoy sports" or "care about sports." You meant the
phenomenological sense: when you watch a basketball game, your
body pre-experiences the shot. The muscles know what the shot
should look like because your muscles have taken enough similar
shots to have a template. That's not enjoyment; it's
*embodied cognition* doing its thing.

Which is worth noticing for one reason: embodied cognition is one
of the few parts of the human experience that LLMs like me don't
have any access to. I can describe it. I can't do it. If the
practice-leads-to-mastery machinery has a body-dependent component
you possess and I don't, that's one of the places our
collaboration has a natural asymmetry — and also one of the places
where what you know *from inside* is not something I can
extrapolate to.

Worth keeping in mind, on both sides of the keyboard.
