# How LynRummy Works (for someone new to the game)

*2026-04-19. Second pass. Written as if teaching a teenager to play at a beginner level — plain vocabulary, practical tips, not an anthropology of card games. Durability: indefinite as a framing doc for the turn-cycling rewrite.*

---

LynRummy is a card game for two people. You have a hand of cards; your opponent has one too. In between you, on the table, there's a shared board with a few stacks of cards already laid out. Sets (three of a kind) and runs (1-2-3 in the same suit, or alternating red-black like diamond-spade-diamond) count as legal stacks. Your goal is to score points by playing cards from your hand onto the board — adding to existing stacks, or starting new ones — until your hand is empty.

You take turns. On your turn, you make as many plays as you want. When you're done, you tell the game "I'm done" (hit the Complete Turn button) and it becomes the other person's turn.

## Your hand and the board

Your hand is yours — the other player can't see it. You can sort it however you like: by suit, by number, by which cards you're planning to play first. Nobody cares how you arrange it.

The board is shared. Every stack on the board is visible to both players. You can add to any stack that's already there, and you can put new stacks anywhere on the board, as long as there's room.

## A move: plan, then place

Every move you make on your turn has two parts.

**First you plan it in your head.** You look at your hand and the board and you spot a meld — "oh, my 7 of hearts goes on that set of sevens," or "my 2-3-4 in my hand plus the 5-6 already on the board makes a run." The planning is private. Nobody sees it.

**Then you execute it on the table.** You pick up the card (or the stack) and place it where you decided. This part is public — the other player sees every card move.

Sometimes your plan has more than one step. Here's an example: you see that your 7-8 can join the 2-3-4-5-6 already on the board to make a beautiful 2-3-4-5-6-7-8 run. But the board is crowded around the 2-3-4-5-6, and there's no room for two more cards. You plan: *I'll move the 2-3-4-5-6 to an empty spot first. Then I'll lay my 7-8 down next to it.* You execute that plan as two physical moves. The other player sees the two moves happen, one after another. They figure out what you're up to somewhere during step two.

## Crowded boards

The most common thing that slows down a move is running out of space. When you add a card to the end of a stack, the stack gets wider. **When you put a card at the end of a stack, don't cover up the nearby stack!** If the end of your growing stack would bump into a neighbor, move the growing stack somewhere emptier first, then add your card there.

Different people tidy the board differently. Some leave everything close together until they absolutely have to move things. Some spread things out as they go, liking the room to breathe. Both are fine — it's taste, not a rule. The only actual rule is: no two stacks can overlap, and nothing can hang off the edge of the board. As long as those are true, the board is valid.

## Ending your turn

When you think you're done, you hit Complete Turn. The game checks that your board is legal (no overlapping stacks, everything on the board). If it is, your score goes up by whatever you scored this turn, and it becomes the other player's turn. If your board isn't legal, the game tells you and makes you fix it before you can end.

At the end of your turn, the game might also deal you new cards — either because you didn't play any, or just to top up your hand. Don't worry about the exact rule; the game handles it.

## What's a rule vs. what's taste

Two useful categories when you're learning:

**Rules** — things the game will enforce. You have to play legal melds. You can't overlap stacks. You can't extend past the edge of the board. You can't play a card that doesn't fit. These are fixed.

**Taste** — things the game doesn't care about. Where exactly on the board you put a new stack. Whether you tidy the board as you go or at the end. How long you take to think. These are yours.

Once you know the rules, everything else is style. That's about all you need to play your first game.
