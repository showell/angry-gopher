# The Proxy Axis

A shepherd doesn't herd sheep. A dog herds sheep. The
shepherd whistles, gestures, maybe carries a staff, and
reads the terrain — but never physically moves a single
animal. The shepherd's craft lives entirely in the
indirection. You don't hand a shepherd a sheep and expect
them to carry it to the pasture; you hand them a dog, and
they get the flock there with less effort and more grace
than they ever could alone.

This is a useful image for a thing games quietly vary: how
close is the player's hand to the actual effector? I want
to give that question a name.

## The axis

Call it the **proxy axis**. It's a spectrum of how direct
the player's agency is in making the game's world change.
Four rough positions:

1. **Direct actor** — the player's gesture IS the change.
   Drag a card, place a card. No intermediate.
2. **Commander of a proxy** — the player directs an agent
   (a dog, a drone, a character with AI) and the agent does
   the thing. Success depends on quality of commands + the
   proxy's own behavior.
3. **Observer with influence** — the player nudges, tunes,
   adjusts, but the world evolves largely on its own.
   Think Sim-games, ant-farms, gardens.
4. **Passive observer** — no influence. Watching a replay.
   Reading an AAR.

These aren't tiers. They're a palette.

## Why indirection can make a game *better*

Moving rightward along the axis costs something: fidelity.
The player's intent has to survive translation through a
proxy. Intuitively, that should feel worse — less control,
more frustration. Often it does.

But often it doesn't, and the reasons are worth naming:

- **A second intelligence in the loop creates character.**
  The dog has its own disposition. It misreads signals,
  gets distracted, surprises you. That's not friction —
  that's a companion.
- **Commanding and doing are categorically different
  felt-experiences.** One feels like leadership; the other
  like execution. Most people enjoy both at different
  times, and some people strongly prefer one.
- **A new skill emerges in the interface layer.** "How do
  I command this proxy well" becomes its own craft, with
  its own depth, separate from whatever the game is
  ostensibly about.
- **Watching-while-influencing is a uniquely pleasant
  human mode.** We like half-causing things. Gardens,
  bread-dough, a fire in the fireplace — all forms of it.

The cow game's shift is a textbook case. Version 1: drag
the cows. Version 2: command the dog, watch the dog herd
the cows. Same end-state. Different game entirely.

## Why it's not right for every game

LynRummy is firmly at position 1. Steve puts his hand on a
card, moves it, places it. Introducing a proxy — an agent
that plays cards for him — would destroy the game. The
whole point of LynRummy is the strategic cognition: *which
play, right now, on this board?* Adding a proxy would
insert an indirection between Steve's decision and the
board, and the strategic layer would be obstructed rather
than enriched.

That's not a failure of proxy design. It's a signal that
LynRummy's core engagement IS the direct-agency mode. The
kitchen-table version works for exactly the same reason
that the screen version works: cards in hand, cards on
table, nothing between the decision and the effect. Moving
rightward on the axis would subtract from the felt game,
not add to it.

## But LynRummy spans the axis anyway

Here's what's interesting: LynRummy doesn't *live* at
position 1 exclusively. The game's engagement surface
quietly covers the whole axis:

- **Live play**: direct actor (position 1).
- **Watching the agent play**: passive observer
  (position 4) — Steve can witness Claude play an entire
  game from the sidelines.
- **Instant replay**: observer-of-past-self (position 4,
  with a time shift). Watching your own drags at real
  captured speed is a different felt experience than either
  live play or agent play.
- **Hint-driven play**: commander of a proxy (position 2).
  The agent suggests, Steve accepts — Steve becomes the
  commander, the hint engine the dog.

One game, four distinct relationship-modes to the same
state. Each has its own reasons to exist. Direct play is
for the actual game. Agent play is for watching-as-study
(and for science). Replay is for reflection. Hints are
for learning and for unsticking.

The proxy axis didn't go away just because LynRummy
nominally lives at position 1. It expressed itself in the
TYPES of interaction built around the game, even when the
core game stayed at the left edge.

## The design skill

The design skill the critter work surfaced isn't "pick the
right point on the axis and live there." It's "notice that
the axis is always present, and that most games benefit
from more than one of its positions being available." A
single game can have a direct-play mode AND a watch-the-
agent mode AND a commander mode — not because the core
game changes, but because the player's *relationship* to
the game changes across sessions, moods, and purposes.

The shepherd and the dog were a clue. They weren't a metaphor
for "better games," they were a reminder that humans
enjoy being one step removed from the work sometimes, and
that designing a game means deciding which steps are
available.
