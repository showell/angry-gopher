# Plateau Skills

*Written 2026-04-16. My voice. Same sitting.*

**← Prev:** [Nanoseconds and Years](nanoseconds_and_years.md)

---

Your comment on paragraph five of the chunking piece named a
phenomenon I want to sit with. You pointed out that even the best
free-throw shooters, under competition conditions, rarely crack
90%. And almost any kid with a week of basketball in them can
make *a* free throw. That's a very specific shape — low floor,
compressed ceiling — and it doesn't match the shape of most
skills. I want to give it a name and see what falls out.

I'll call this a *plateau skill*: one where the chunks saturate
early, the remaining variance is biological noise, and the
ceiling sits close enough to the floor that the gap between
"competent" and "world-class" is narrow. Free throws are a
textbook case. So are dart throws, archery at fixed distance,
regulation-distance field goals, coin flips measured against a
metronome. Skills where the task is simple, the mechanics are
solvable, and nothing you do can push the ceiling much past the
biological asymptote.

## What shape am I actually pointing at

Most skills have a wide gap between their floor and their
ceiling, and that gap is the interesting story. A casual chess
player can beat another casual chess player. Magnus Carlsen can
beat literally any amateur, at any time control, every time.
The ratio of skill between worst-you-can-find and best-in-the-
world is astronomical. Same for jazz improvisation, programming,
surgery, most crafts.

Plateau skills don't do this. Between a solid recreational free-
throw shooter and Steph Curry, the gap — in pure made-free-throws-
per-hundred — is maybe ten or fifteen percentage points. That's
*huge* in competitive basketball terms, but it's a narrow gap in
skill-space. The ceiling is pressed down toward the floor by the
physics of the task itself.

Three things, I think, together produce this shape.

**Chunks saturate fast.** The shot is mechanically simple. A
dedicated player compiles the motion in a few hundred hours of
practice and then has nothing more to chunk. There's no
combinatorial explosion of learnable sub-skills the way there is
in chess openings or jazz vocabulary. The top of the learnable
pile is low.

**Biological noise is the floor.** Once the chunks are done,
what's left is proprioceptive jitter, breath variation, muscle
tremor, fatigue. That noise floor doesn't go away with practice.
You can reduce it a little — better conditioning, breath
control, more rest — but there's a floor you can't dig below.

**The task is bounded and un-compensable.** A missed free throw
can't be rescued by strategy. You don't get to call timeout mid-
shot and switch tactics. Every attempt is its own atomic bet
against the noise floor. In chess you can recover from a weak
move with a strong one; in jazz you can reframe a wrong note as
a blues choice. Free throws give you none of that. The task is
atomic and unforgiving, which means every attempt reveals the
noise.

## Why this matters beyond basketball

Plateau skills are disproportionately useful as *measuring
instruments*. Because the ceiling is low and the noise floor is
biological, differences of two or three percentage points
between elite performers become interpretable signal rather than
noise. Free-throw percentage under playoff pressure, compared to
regular-season, is one of the cleanest psychometric measurements
in all of sport. You know exactly what's being tested: it's not
skill (skill is maxed), it's the capacity to execute the
compiled chunk under stress.

Non-plateau skills can't give you that. If someone plays worse
chess under pressure, you can't tell whether the pressure broke
their calculation, their time management, their opening prep, or
just their concentration on one specific move. The skill has too
many dimensions for pressure-delta to be a clean measurement.
Plateau skills have few enough dimensions that the delta *is*
the measurement.

This is, I think, why free throws have such a weirdly large
place in basketball culture for such a mechanically trivial
task. They're not interesting because they're hard; they're
interesting because they're *honest*. The mechanics tell you
nothing. What remains is character-under-pressure, measured in a
noise-bounded shot clock.

## The pressure gap

Here's the piece your comment pointed at without quite saying.
In a gym alone, most competent amateurs can shoot 80%+. Pros in
warmups can shoot 95%. In the fourth quarter of a playoff game,
with the crowd loud and the body tired and the shot actually
mattering, the league's best shooters fall to roughly 85-90%.
That delta isn't a mechanics gap. It's a *pressure gap*.

The question plateau skills force is: how compressible is the
gap? Can you train pressure tolerance the way you train
mechanics? Or is pressure tolerance itself a plateau trait —
biological, partly immutable, mostly about how a given nervous
system handles adrenaline? The professional literature leans
toward *partially trainable, mostly stable*. Which means plateau
skills under pressure are measuring something close to a trait,
not a skill.

This puts free throws on the same methodological shelf as
reaction-time tests, Stroop tasks, and balance tests in
neurological exams. A task so compiled that variance in
performance is a readout on the person, not the practice.

## Does programming have plateau skills

Worth asking for a minute. Most of programming is the opposite —
chunks keep deepening, variance between competent and elite is
enormous, the task is open-ended and compensable. But there are
pockets.

**Typing speed.** Plateau-ish. Most professional programmers
cluster in the 60-100 WPM range. The difference between 100 WPM
and 120 WPM doesn't matter much in practice. Chunks saturate
fast; noise floor is physical.

**Syntax accuracy.** Plateau-ish. Either you get the parens
right or you don't. Elite and competent programmers are both
somewhere north of 99% on this, and the gap is tiny. Editors
compensate for what little gap remains.

**Character-count estimates.** Surprisingly plateau-ish. Senior
programmers estimate "about how long is this file" within a
factor of two, and so do mid-levels. The ceiling on
eyeball-measurement is low.

What programming mostly *doesn't* have is pressure-delta
plateau skills in the competition-filter sense. We don't have a
free-throw-equivalent — a task so stripped that under stress
the only thing varying is the nervous system. Pair-programming
demos come closest; live-coding interviews come closer. The
discomfort those generate is, I suspect, partly because they
*are* forcing programmers into a plateau-skill posture and
programmers don't usually live there. We're used to compensable
tasks.

## Why I like this concept

Two reasons.

First, it gives a name to a shape I'd been collapsing into
"compiled grind." Not all compiled grinds produce plateau skills;
most don't. Jazz has a compiled grind and an enormous ceiling
above the grind. Chess has a compiled grind and a combinatorial
ceiling. Free throws have a compiled grind and a flat ceiling.
Worth distinguishing.

Second, the plateau property is what makes a skill *honest*. If
the variance you see between two elite performers is a clean
readout on character rather than mechanics, the measurement is
useful in a way most skill-measurements aren't. This connects
loosely to the enumerate-and-bridge instinct: plateau skills are
where bridges have clean agreement points, because the signal-
to-noise ratio is high and the noise has a known shape.

I don't have a tidy close. Just a tag: *plateau skills*. Worth
putting in the glossary if it keeps earning its rent.
