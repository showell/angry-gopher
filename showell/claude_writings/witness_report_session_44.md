# Witness Report — First Moves of a Game

*2026-04-19. Observing Steve's play in session 44. Kitchen-table narration, physical beats where they happened, logical move where those beats added up to one. I didn't see the table; I'm narrating from the action log plus Steve's context that he played steadily and without rabbit-holing.*

---

Steve sat down to a fresh board. The standard opening stacks were in place — a 4-card spade run, a 4-card diamond run, the heart run 2-3-4, the 7-set (7♠-7♦-7♣), the ace set, and the long rainbow run ending at 7♥. A 15-card hand was his.

## Move 1 — Assemble the hearts run from hand

Steve pulled the **8 of hearts** out of his hand and placed it on an empty spot toward the right side of the board. He followed it with the **7 of hearts**, landing it just left of the 8 — now a two-card beginning. Then the **6 of hearts**, also on the left. Three cards from his hand, built up into a **6H-7H-8H** run right out in the open, in three quick physical beats. Whole move took about a second of table time.

## Move 2 — Nudge the new hearts run over

He picked up the freshly-built 6-7-8 and slid it to a calmer spot, center-left. This is the kind of move he flagged earlier — cosmetic, not logistical. The run had just been built in an off-to-the-side drop zone; he wanted it somewhere he could see it.

## Move 3 — Extend the spade run

He reached into his hand for the **Queen of Spades** and dropped it on the left end of the existing K-A-2-3 spade run. Now the stack was **Q-K-A-2-3**. One card, one beat, one clean extension. The spade run had started this game four cards long and was now five.

## Move 4 — Re-home the extended spade run

With the run one card wider, he repositioned it up to the top of the board. This is the pattern you see after most extensions — the act of growing pushes the right edge out, and sometimes the old spot doesn't fit the new width, or just doesn't feel right anymore. Either way, he settled it higher up.

## Move 5 — Slide the ace set down

Pure tidying. Steve moved the **A♣-A♦-A♥** set — which he hadn't touched — to a new position on the lower-left of the board. Nothing was about to happen on it. Just organizing.

## Move 6 — Slide the 7-set down

Same kind of move. He moved the **7♠-7♦-7♣** set to the lower portion of the board, near where he'd put the aces. Probably lining things up into a row he could scan easily.

## Move 7 — Play the 8 of clubs

He dropped the **8♣** from his hand onto the right end of the stack that had been the 7-set. Whatever the resulting structure is — Steve's in charge of the legality; I'm just the witness — this was a one-card play, one beat, and he moved on.

## Move 8 — Play the 4 of spades

Similar shape. The **4♠** from his hand went onto the right end of the big rainbow run. One card, one beat.

## Move 9 — A burst of tidying

Several moves in close succession: he repositioned the rainbow stack, the ace set, the 2-3-4 heart run, and the diamond run, one after another. The diamond run in particular he moved **four separate times** — four consecutive repositionings of the same stack, no plays in between. That's the unmistakable signature of someone trying a spot, not liking it, trying another, settling. By the end the board was visibly organized into rough columns — his visual field tidied up the way he likes it.

## Move 10 — Complete turn

He signaled the turn over. Hand smaller by five cards (the three hearts, the 8♣, the 4♠), board rearranged, spade run extended.

## What I noticed

Most of the physical beats in this first turn were **tidying**, not plays. By my rough count: five plays from hand, seventeen physical moves total, so twelve of those were board organization. That's not inefficient — it's the shape of how Steve plays. Cards come out of hand, land somewhere, and he relocates them into the grid he maintains in his head. No rabbit holes, no long deliberation on any single move, just a steady flow of "play, tidy, play, tidy."

The cosmetic-vs-logistical distinction you flagged earlier is easy to see in the log when it's **four-in-a-row on the same stack** (clearly cosmetic) and easy to see when it's a **single move right after an extension** (clearly logistical). The middle ground — a tidy move of a stack nobody just touched — reads as cosmetic in your case, and it matches what you described: keeping things in your visual field, generally toward center.
